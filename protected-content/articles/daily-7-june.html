1 ECONOMY

7.8% growth spurt in FY26 despite West Asia turmoil

GDPQ4 FY26

India expanded 7.8% in the March quarter, beating expectations and suggesting the West Asia crisis had only a limited effect, provisional data show. Full-year FY26 growth is pegged at 7.7%, above the RBI's 7% estimate and the 7.6% forecast by economists.

Prime Minister Narendra Modi said the momentum reflected "the efforts of 1.4 billion Indians," while Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman credited the "Reform Express" and commitment to fiscal targets.

Drivers:

  • Investment surge: Gross fixed capital formation up 10.8% in Q4 (vs 4.9% in Q3)
  • Private final consumption expenditure rose 4.0% in Q4
  • Government spending up 6.2% in Q4
  • Double-digit growth for five consecutive quarters in hotels and transport sector

Sectoral: Agriculture 5.4%, construction 8.4%, electricity 5.4%, financial and professional services accelerated to 6.4% in Q4. Manufacturing slowed to 3.5% after posting double-digit growth for five quarters. Officials cautioned the fallout from West Asia could impact FY27 growth.

Indian economy slips to 6th spot on rupee weakness

Global RankingCurrency

India's economy expanded to $3.46 trillion in FY26, slightly higher than earlier estimates, with nominal GDP growing at a 10.8% CAGR between FY21-FY26. Yet India slipped to become the world's sixth-largest economy in dollar terms, behind the US, China, Germany, Japan and the UK.

The reason: rupee depreciation. As the rupee weakened steadily against the dollar, the value of India's output eroded when converted to dollars, limiting gains in global rankings despite strong domestic growth.

Currency moves 2025 (Y-o-Y): India rupee -4.26%, Japan yen -1.13%, China yuan -0.28%, UK pound +0.76%, Euro +0.89%. The rupee fell 80.37 to 87.29 per dollar between 2021-2024, and to 88.31 in 2025.

Domestic growth remains strong — GDP at current prices rose from ₹229.8 lakh crore (2021) to ₹346.4 lakh crore (2025) — but dollar conversion masks the strength.

2 TECHNOLOGY & CYBERSECURITY

Govt's cybersecurity efforts get Claude Mythos boost

Anthropic5 June

The Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre (I4C), Cert-In, NCIIPC and the Digital Intelligence Platform (DIP) are among a handful of central agencies likely to get limited access to Anthropic's Claude Mythos for testing and deployment.

Some Indian IT and ITes firms with small AI engineering teams will also get access. Claude Mythos, Anthropic's latest general-purpose model, has demonstrated strong capabilities in locating dormant bugs and exploiting loopholes in old software systems.

I4C (MHA) is the nodal agency to fight cybercrime; Cert-In (MeitY) is the nodal agency for cyber security; NCIIPC protects critical infrastructure including nuclear power plants.

The move follows Anthropic's expansion of Project Glasswing to India and 14 other countries, weeks after Nasscom and MeitY sought access.

RBI issues advisory: "We remain fully prepared in terms of handling cyber security threats of this nature as well as conventional threats" — Deputy Governor Swaminathan J

RBI said it is "mindfully prepared" for Mythos-related threats and has issued advisories to regulated entities, engaging at government and financial-sector inter-regulatory forum levels.

3 OPINION

Why missing the AI boom may not be so bad

R JagannathanBeyond Ideology

India's absence from the AI valuation frenzy could prove a blessing once risk-aversion sets in, argues R Jagannathan. The AI boom, like Tulip Mania and the dotcom bubble, is essentially speculative — bound to deflate, though timing is uncertain.

Key arguments:

  • Even the US economy ($32 tn) is not big enough to justify current valuations — the world's 14 trillion-dollar companies are worth $25 tn
  • SpaceX's IPO is underway, potentially valuing it at $1.77 tn (making Musk the first trillionaire), but valuation guru Aswath Damodaran pegs it closer to $1.2 tn
  • 12 of 14 trillion-dollar firms are tech companies — lopsidedness that will correct
  • Financial wealth ($600 tn globally) has outpaced real GDP since 2000; top 1% hold at least 20% of wealth

McKinsey warns three possible outcomes: a global balance-sheet reset, asset price deflation, or sharp collapse with high inflation and low growth. Since AI leads the boom, the bust will also be AI-led, causing job destruction and a regulatory nightmare over taxing robots.

Jagannathan's prescription: governments should keep "powder dry," help victims, and focus on sovereign AI models that can be regulated at home rather than chasing FOMO-driven valuations. "If India has been left out of the boom, it will also be spared some of the consequences of the bust."
