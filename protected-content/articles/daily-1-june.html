Fuel price rise drives spike in EV penetration in May

EV31 May

Soaring petrol prices after the prolonged Hormuz blockade pushed electric two-wheeler penetration to 9.12% in May — up from 7.8% in April and just over 6% in May 2024 — nearing double digits for the first time.

VAHAN data: e2W sales jumped 58.6% YoY to 159,925 units (vs 100,772), while total two-wheeler registrations grew only 4.6%.

Market share (May): TVS 25.9% (leader), Bajaj 23.7%, Ather 17.2%, Hero 11.2%, Ola Electric 9.22% (improved MoM but still <10%).

Electric LMVs hit 6.9% of passenger vehicle registrations (24,677 of 354,691), up from 4.7% last year. Tata Motors leads e-PV with >38% share, followed by Mahindra (5,644 units) and JSW MG (4,790). Maruti's sole e-Vitara logged ~1,500 units; VinFast neared 1,000 with 908.

Reimagining microfinance

M S SriramOpinion

The joint-liability group model — inspired by Grameen Bank — works, but only at modest growth, argues IIM-B professor M S Sriram. Built for unsecured working-capital loans to women (interest >24%), it unlocked spare household labour but was never designed for poverty eradication or large capex.

Crises are geographic: when regions saturate, multiple lending, coercive recovery and usurious rates follow. Demonetisation and Covid broke group liability and meeting discipline, yet MFIs only tinkered — adding graduation loans and digital collections — without reimagining the model.

Sriram flags RBI's 50% household-income repayment cap as "impossible to measure and too liberal," inviting stress. The recent move to allow 40% of MFI books outside this definition helps diversify balance sheets, but operating models remain unchanged.

Way forward: build "meso-finance" for graduates needing larger, longer-tenor loans at lower rates without group meetings; focus on savings buffers, not just credit guarantees. The next crisis, he warns, may come from data-driven fintechs lending without client interface.

West Asia war: Lessons for India

DefenceGeopolitics

Indian military leaders say the US-Israel-Iran war — where Iran held off superior forces for weeks — offers stark lessons for India: prepare for multi-front, multi-year conflicts; prioritise low-cost weapons over expensive missiles; and build ISR and intelligence to avert failures.

Air Marshal S P Dharkar (retd): "This is not a war that a modern power would be able to sustain" — the Pentagon spent ~$3bn in 15 days. India must be ready for simultaneous wars across domains, with strategic self-reliance in supply chains.

Key takeaways:

  • Clearly define political and military objectives — avoid mismatches seen in Operation Sindoor
  • Invest in cheap, mass-produced drones vs $2m+ missiles; build stockpiles for sustained war
  • Strengthen maritime posture beyond eastward focus; expand SSN fleet (6 planned by 2036)
  • Accelerate AI-enabled sensor fusion, algorithmic warfare, space surveillance
  • Boost domestic defence R&D (currently <6% of defence spend) and private-sector manufacturing

Former Navy chief Admiral R Hari Kumar noted India needs both hard power for sea lanes and soft power for commerce, while building a reliable industrial base to avoid dependence during crises.
