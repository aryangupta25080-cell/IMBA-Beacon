1 ENERGY SECURITY

Govt directs OMCs to build 30-day LPG stock

MoPNG29 May

The government has directed state-run oil marketing companies to expand LPG storage to meet a minimum 30-day national requirement, joint secretary Sujata Sharma said at an inter-ministerial briefing.

"We are working on strategic reserves. We have asked OMCs to maintain LPG reserves at minimum of 30 days. Similarly, we are working for crude oil," she said.

India faces a more pronounced LPG shortage than crude because roughly 90% of LPG imports transit the Strait of Hormuz. India currently maintains 60 days of crude and 45 days of LPG stocks.

The government also flagged unusually high petrol and diesel sales as bulk consumers shift to retail outlets, urging states to form special squads under the Essential Commodities Act to curb blackmarketing and diversion.

Separately, the India-bound crude tanker Nissos Keros (270,000 tonnes, HPCL charter) safely crossed Hormuz on 26 May and is expected at Visakhapatnam on 3 June. Officials said 13 Indian-flagged vessels, including one LPG carrier and five crude tankers, remain stranded near Hormuz.

Muddled petroleum-pricing regime

OpinionAjay Tyagi

Former petroleum secretary Ajay Tyagi argues the current crisis exposes India's failure to implement market-based pricing announced in 2002. With oil and gas still ~31% of the energy basket and import dependence at 85% and 50%, India remains a price-taker, accounting for 12% of global crude imports.

The government faces a trilemma: keep prices low, protect tax revenues, and contain the forex outgo. PSU OMCs cannot absorb losses indefinitely, and the Centre has limited fiscal space.

THE INCREASE IN DOMESTIC PRICES SHOULD HAVE BEEN EFFECTED GRADUALLY, IN SMALLER INSTALMENTS, SPREAD OVER A LONGER PERIOD OF, SAY, TWO TO THREE MONTHS

Tyagi calls for a principle-based SoP: during high prices, Centre and states should take a tax hit; during low prices, raise taxes. He urges reviving BPCL privatisation to reduce government pressure and allow true market pricing.

2 ECONOMY & TECHNOLOGY

India needs to build $150 bn semicon value chain by 2035: NITI

NITI AayogSemiconductors

NITI Aayog's "Future of India's Semiconductor Industry" report sets a target of a $120-150 billion domestic value chain by 2035, prioritising leadership over participation.

India spent almost $150 billion on semiconductor imports during FY17-FY25, with imports growing at 23% CAGR. At this pace, the annual bill could hit $240 billion by 2035.

Key recommendations:

  • Government to fund at least one-third of the $135-180 billion cumulative investment needed over the next decade
  • Make India a top-3 global destination for OSAT and advanced packaging
  • Five-pillar approach: pioneering, policy and investment, production, people, partnership

The report urges India to skip the foundry catch-up race and win the "More-than-Moore" era — focusing on advanced packaging, system integration and scale. Challenges include global supply chain entrenchment and acceptance of made-in-India chips.

Dismantle nuclear capacity, open Hormuz: Trump to Iran

US-Iran29 May

President Donald Trump said he would decide Friday in the Situation Room on a deal to extend the ceasefire with Iran for 60 days. The deal would require Iran to "never have a Nuclear Weapon or Bomb" and for Hormuz to be "immediately open, no tolls, for unrestricted shipping."

Oil prices fell and stocks rose on the news. Trump posted that mines would be removed and trapped ships could go home: "Say Hello to your wives, husbands, parents, and families from me, your favorite President!"

He added: "NO MONEY WILL BE EXCHANGED, UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE. OTHER ITEMS, OF FAR LESS IMPORTANCE, HAVE BEEN AGREED TO" — a pushback on Iran's demands for tolls, reparations and frozen funds.

Iran's chief negotiator Mohammad Baqer Qalibaf said: "We do not trust guarantees and words, only actions are the criterion." Pakistan's Ishaq Dar arrived in Washington to mediate. Kazakhstan has offered to take Iran's near-weapons-grade uranium stockpile if a deal is reached.

3 CLIMATE & AGRICULTURE

El Nino effect: IMD lowers 2026 monsoon forecast

IMDMonsoon 2026

IMD has lowered its 2026 southwest monsoon forecast to 90% of long-period average (from 92% in April), signalling "below normal" rains due to El Niño development. The Indian Ocean Dipole is expected to remain neutral.

No part of the country except the Northeast is expected to get "normal" rainfall. The monsoon core zone — central and western India, home to most rain-fed pulses, oilseeds and sugarcane — is likely to receive less than 94% of LPA.

June rainfall is also forecast below normal at 92% of LPA, potentially delaying paddy and maize sowing. Above-normal maximum temperatures are likely in June over most of India.

Agriculture Minister Shivraj Singh Chouhan said the Centre has a "war chest" of 174,000 quintals of seeds over and above the 1.73 million tonnes required for kharif, with total availability at 1.93 mt.
