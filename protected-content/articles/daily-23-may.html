1 RUPEE & FOREX INTERVENTION

₹ stages two-day comeback to 95.69 after RBI blitz

RupeeIntervention22 May

After flirting with 97 on Monday-Tuesday, the rupee closed Friday at 95.69, up 0.53% on the day and 1.2% across Thursday-Friday. Dealers estimate RBI sold $2-3 billion each day via large state-run banks in both onshore spot and offshore NDF markets.

"RBI has been intervening aggressively... with a lot of intent and managed to crush short-term speculators," — Abhishek Goenka, IFA Global

The move coincided with Brent easing to ~$104 from $112 earlier in the week after reports of a US-Iran draft peace deal and hopes for Strait of Hormuz reopening. HDFC Securities' Dilip Parmar noted the rupee "maintained upward momentum for second straight session... alongside reprieve in imported commodity prices."

Why it matters: Technical resistance now sits at 96.20, support at 95.40. Despite the weekly gain of 0.29%, the rupee is still down 0.81% in May, 6.08% in 2026 YTD, and 10.12% YoY — underscoring that intervention buys time, not trend reversal.

RBI net-sold record $53.13 bn in FY26

FY26Dollar SalesRecord

RBI sold a net $53.13 billion in the spot market in FY26 — the highest in any financial year — after buying $142 bn and selling $195 bn. It followed FY25's net sale of $34.51 bn.

Monthly peaks: October ($11.88 bn), December ($10.02 bn), March ($9.76 bn, third-highest). RBI was a net buyer in January (+$7.41 bn) and February before returning to sales in March.

To avoid draining rupee liquidity, RBI leaned on forwards: its net short dollar position in forwards jumped to $103.06 bn at end-March from $77.67 bn in February, a rise of $18.72 bn in FY26.

"For the past year, there has been massive FPI outflow, keeping the rupee under pressure, and RBI was there to contain volatility" — treasury head, private bank

Why it matters: The 9.9% rupee depreciation in FY26 happened despite record intervention. With REER falling to 88.06 in April (from 89.23 in March), exports gain competitiveness but import inflation risk rises.

Reserves drop $8 bn; India biggest dollar seller since conflict

ReservesBarclays

Forex reserves fell to $688.9 bn in the week ended May 15 — a near six-week low and an $8 bn weekly drop — as RBI stepped up defence during a 1.55% rupee slide that week.

Barclays ranks India as the largest seller of dollars (spot + forwards) since the West Asia conflict began, followed by China and Philippines. Singapore was the largest buyer, then Malaysia.

Reserves are now $39 bn below the Feb 27 peak of $728 bn. The decline mirrors both spot sales and valuation losses.

Why it matters: At ~$689 bn, import cover remains strong, but the pace of drawdown limits RBI's firepower if oil spikes again and FII outflows persist.

Crude still up 36% since Feb despite pullback

OilBrent

Brent closed Friday at $100.71, down from $112 intra-week but still up 36.3% since late February when the West Asia conflict escalated. The Indian basket remains elevated, forcing OMCs to raise retail prices on May 15 and May 19.

Lower crude helped the rupee this week, but markets await final US-Iran terms on uranium enrichment and a proposed Hormuz toll mechanism.

Why it matters: Every $10 rise in crude adds roughly 0.3-0.4% to India's import bill. With the rupee near 96, the feedback loop between oil and FX stays tight.

2 RBI BALANCE SHEET & SURPLUS

Record ₹2.87 trillion surplus to Centre for FY26

RBIDividendECF

The Central Board (623rd meeting, Mumbai, chaired by Governor Sanjay Malhotra) approved a surplus transfer of ₹2,86,588 crore for FY26, 7% higher than FY25's ₹2,68,590 crore.

Key FY26 numbers: balance sheet expanded 20.61% to ₹91.97 trillion; gross income up 26.42%; expenditure before risk provisioning up 27.60%; net income before provisions at ₹5.96 trillion vs ₹3.13 trillion in FY25.

The Board lowered the Contingent Risk Buffer to 6.5% from 7.5%, within the revised Economic Capital Framework band of 4.5-7.5% (earlier 5.5-6.5%). Provisioning still rose to ₹1.09 trillion.

Why it matters: The transfer equals 90.8% of budgeted non-tax revenue, giving the Centre fiscal room as oil-related pressures build.

Why surplus was below market hopes — economists decode

AnalysisProvisioning

Despite the CRB cut, the surplus undershot some estimates because the 21% balance sheet expansion mechanically lifted provisioning requirements.

"The RBI's surplus transfer was lower than some market estimates largely because provisioning turned out to be higher than expected" — Gaura Sen Gupta, IDFC First Bank

"Despite the CRB ratio being cut... provisioning more than doubled to ₹1.09 trillion due to 21% YoY expansion... strong dividend was likely driven by higher G-Sec interest income and robust forex earnings from $180 bn of forex sales" — Madhavi Arora, Emkay Global

"Transferring higher amount to the CRB will help in RBI intervening... as per evolving domestic and global conditions" — Devendra Kumar Pant, India Ratings

Sen Gupta also flagged a possible negative entry in revaluation accounts that required a debit from the contingency fund — clarity awaited in the annual report.

3 GROWTH, INFLATION & FISCAL STRESS

RBI: 'Rising crude a challenge for external sector'

State of EconomyMay 2026

RBI's monthly report (guided by Deputy Governor Poonam Gupta; views of authors) said India entered this phase from macro strength, with domestic demand the key growth driver, but near-term outlook is "somewhat clouded by supply-side pressures."

Headline CPI edged up to 3.5% in April from 3.4% in March on food. WPI surged to 8.3% in April from 3.9% in March — a 42-month high — led by fuel and power.

The report flagged: (1) inflation pass-through needs monitoring; (2) above-normal minimum temperatures and unseasonal rains risk rabi harvest; (3) merchandise trade deficit widened in April on crude and gold imports.

Positives: robust services exports, positive net FDI, strong reserve buffers, and proactive policy measures by government and RBI.

BoB: West Asia crisis may cost ₹2.1 trillion

Bank of BarodaFiscal

BoB economists see FY27 GDP at 6.5-6.8% but warn fiscal deficit could rise to 4.7-4.8% of GDP vs budgeted 4.3%, a shortfall of ~₹2.1 trillion (deficit up from ₹17 trn to ₹18-18.5 trn).

"If you do want to maintain deficit around 4.3%, your capital expenditure will have to be reduced... expenditure in other ministries will also have to be brought down" — Sonal Badhan, BoB

Fiscal math under strain despite RBI bonanza

ICRAIndia Ratings

Aditi Nayar (ICRA) expects the Centre to miss the FY27 4.3% deficit target by 40 bps assuming $95 crude, citing higher fertiliser and fuel subsidies plus lower tax collections and OMC dividends.

Even with the ₹2.87 trn RBI transfer, the budget faces pressure from three sources identified by BoB, plus rupee depreciation lifting the import bill.

"We are looking at a fairly stable performance of the Indian economy, notwithstanding the war effects, but definitely at a micro level... industry level there will be a lot of concerns" — Madan Sabnavis, Chief Economist, BoB

Why it matters: The RBI dividend cushions, but does not eliminate, the need for either higher borrowing or capex cuts if oil stays near $100-105.

Policy outlook: RBI to stay on hold, watch data

Monetary PolicyBoB View

BoB expects RBI to maintain status quo on rates and remain in "wait-and-watch" mode, given only April CPI is available and the full impact of the crisis is yet to be assessed.

Rupee forecast: 97-98 per dollar in coming months, driven by FII outflows and a dollar that has strengthened 1.5% since February.

Inflation pass-through: CPI basket impact from oil seen at 1.2-2.0% annualised; WPI impact sharper at 2.3-3.7% as firms absorb costs, squeezing margins before passing through.

Why it matters: With WPI at 8.3% and CPI creeping up, RBI's tolerance for further FX intervention without tightening liquidity will be tested.
