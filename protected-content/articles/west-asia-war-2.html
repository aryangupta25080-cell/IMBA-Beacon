West Asia war: Lessons for India

Geopolitics Defence Strategic Lessons

Until recently, few analysts would have argued that Iran could hold off the combined might of the United States and a technologically superior Israeli armed forces for more than two weeks. The war has forced the US, Israel, India and even Pakistan to reassess assumptions about modern warfare.

Current and former Indian officers say India must draw several lessons: prepare to pre-empt multi-front, prolonged wars; shop clever and use low-cost weapons; build ISR (awareness, deterrence, surveillance, reconnaissance); and invest heavily in intelligence to avert failure.

"This is not a war that a modern power would be able to sustain" — Air Marshal S P Dharkar (retd), former Vice-Chief, Indian Air Force

The Pentagon has already spent close to $3 billion in the first 15 days, including $2.5 billion for base repairs. The cost trajectory underscores why sustainability, not just firepower, will decide future conflicts.

1. Objectives and Strategic Alignment

Indian forces remain largely border-focused, with maritime assets positioned eastward. Former Navy Chief Admiral R Hari Kumar argues India must now think through the West Asia conflict as a geopolitical actor, not just a regional power.

Core imperatives:

  • Define ends clearly: Do not enter war without matching political aims to military means. The US entered without a clear exit; regime change was never defined.
  • Avoid mismatch: India's Operation Sindoor (summer 2025) is cited internally as a case where political and military objectives were not aligned.
  • Use geography strategically: Hard power is presence and mobility; soft power is securing sea lines of communication for energy and trade.
  • Plan for multi-domain war: simultaneous kinetic, sub-threshold, cyber, and disaster-management scenarios.

Dharkar stressed "strategic self-reliance" — depending on foreign suppliers during crises would be catastrophic. The military is now reviewing its "inventory backlog" for prolonged wars, including non-traditional industries and reliable supply chains.

2. Weapons: Cost, Scale and Self-Reliance

Both sides have expended missiles and drones at unsustainable rates. US missiles often cost over $2 million each, rapidly depleting stockpiles.

India's next war is not about winning, it is about sustaining — senior officer

Lessons for India:

  • Shop clever, buy cheap: Produce large numbers of low-cost attack drones and loitering munitions rather than relying solely on expensive precision-guided weapons used in Operation Sindoor.
  • Build depth: Both US and Israel have had to restock mid-conflict. India, with Russian-origin dependence, would struggle against China or the US in a long war.
  • Closed-loop sustainment: War is a combination of autonomous unmanned systems, advanced air power, and industrial depth.
  • Diversify suppliers: Develop multiple weapon types — precision, swarm, and long-range air defence — to avoid single-point failures.

3. Maritime and Energy Security

India has stationed ~20 warships and dozens of vessels in the Indian Ocean Region, including a submarine deployment 20 nautical miles off Galle, Sri Lanka — demonstrating sea denial and energy-route protection.

Yet the Navy's nuclear-powered attack submarine (SSN) fleet remains limited. India aims to build six SSNs by 2036 to operate far from shores. Despite the war, crude supplies have not stopped, proving the value of diversified sourcing and naval presence.

4. Technology, ISR and Algorithmic Warfare

The conflict has shattered the assumption that air power alone wins wars. Instead, victory hinged on platforms, networked sensor grids, and AI-enhanced data fusion that compressed sensor-to-shooter timelines.

Key shifts:

  • Algorithmic warfare: predictive AI, composite common operating picture, extensive software-defined operations
  • Space and surveillance: Lt Gen Gurmeet Singh (retd) notes future war will be shaped by space-based ISR, "Star Wars"-like assets, and AI coding tools
  • Perception battle: Rear Admiral Raja Menon (retd) warns India must invest in perception planning for social media era, not just kinetic operations
  • Priorities: India last year prioritised AEW&C and air-to-air refuellers; now needs to accelerate networked grids and electronic warfare

Wing Commander Vikas Kalyanaraman (CAPS) observes that the war ended with a political statement about outdated doctrine, not the limits of air power — modern data fusion enables surgical strikes at scale.

5. Industrial Base and Research Funding

DRDO remains India's primary defence developer, but military R&D is below 6% of defence spending. SIPRI ranks India as the world's fifth-largest military spender in 2025; Pakistan hit a record $3.8 billion.

Colonel Ramesh Singh (retd), VIF, argues: "Our industrial capacity must be factored in the military plan. The government and private sector in the post-West Asia world must work together to ensure a strong defence industry."

Without a reliable supply chain and solid industrial base, India cannot sustain a long war — a lesson reinforced by US and Israeli restocking needs.

Summary: Five Pillars for India

  1. Pre-emption and endurance: Plan for multi-front, prolonged wars; review inventory for sustainability.
  2. Clear objectives: Align political aims with military means; define exit criteria upfront.
  3. Low-cost mass: Shift from few expensive missiles to many cheap drones and swarm systems.
  4. ISR and AI: Invest in surveillance, reconnaissance, space assets, and algorithmic warfare.
  5. Self-reliance: Build domestic defence industrial base with private sector, reduce import dependence.
