'Crude prices could influence growth, inflation'

EAC-PMOil21 May

The West Asia war will weigh on India's fiscal economy, supply chains and oil market even if it ends soon, S Mahendra Dev, chairman of the Economic Advisory Council to the PM, said at NIPFP's Golden Jubilee conference. "It will take some time for oil production and supply chains to stabilise. And, we may not go back to $69 per barrel in the near future," he noted.

Dev said India's macro position remains strong, but crude could shape FY27 outcomes. Growth could stay in the 6.5-7% range if oil stays up to $95, but prices around $120 would force a fresh re-estimation of growth and inflation.

He called for a structured risk-management framework — identifying "choke points" in energy, food and fertilisers, building strategic petroleum reserves, and accelerating renewables. India has already met two-thirds of its renewable commitments four years early. He also backed tariff support, FTAs and diversification, citing a ₹1 trillion stabilisation fund and ECLGS as proof of improved crisis preparedness.

Why it matters: With the rupee near 97 and Hormuz flows disrupted, sustained $95+ oil threatens India's import bill and fiscal math. Dev's push for buffers signals policy will prioritize resilience over short-term growth — a shift that could mean more strategic reserves and faster renewable rollout, even if it raises near-term costs.

India's Russian oil gains crimped as US changes waiver terms

EnergyRussiaSanctions

Russian oil flows to India are set to fall after the US altered its 30-day waiver extension. While India imported over 2 million bpd for four of the last ten weeks — hitting 2.4 million bpd this week and 1.8 million bpd in April — May loadings have collapsed to below 700,000 bpd amid uncertainty.

The May 18 order only authorizes crude loaded on or before April 17, 2026, through June 17. Unlike prior waivers that rolled the loading date forward, this version does not cover new cargoes. Kpler data show just nine Urals cargoes loaded for India in May versus 23 in April, with very few sanctioned barrels available for June delivery.

Refiners say unsanctioned cargoes from smaller producers remain, and sanctioned sellers are using intermediaries. The $275 million Adani Enterprises settlement with OFAC over alleged Iranian LPG imports has rattled buyers. With Hormuz still restricted, analysts note Russian crude retains a price and logistics edge via non-strait routes.

Why it matters: The waiver tweak directly hits India's cheap crude lifeline just as West Asia risk keeps Brent elevated. Lower Russian volumes will force refiners back toward costlier Middle Eastern grades, pressuring margins and the trade deficit — amplifying the inflation-growth trade-off flagged by EAC-PM.

PM's five-nation tour to bring $40 bn investments: MEA

DiplomacyInvestment21 May

Prime Minister Narendra Modi met over 50 global CEOs during his May 15-20 tour of the UAE, Netherlands, Sweden, Norway and Italy, the MEA said. Fresh commitments, including expansion plans, total nearly $40 billion, with cumulative exposure of these firms in India already around $180 billion.

MEA spokesperson Randhir Jaiswal said projects are in the pipeline across semiconductors, logistics, green infrastructure and technology. In Abu Dhabi on May 15, India signed pacts on strategic petroleum reserves, long-term LNG supply, defence and shipping, with $5 billion pledged.

Commerce Minister Piyush Goyal cited "57 outcomes" — strengthening India's semiconductor mission, energy security and defence manufacturing. The PM also attended the India-Nordic summit in Oslo and, in Rome, set a €20 billion bilateral trade target with Italy by 2029 with PM Giorgia Meloni.

Why it matters: The tour converts geopolitical outreach into capital commitments at a time of West Asia uncertainty. By leveraging FTAs with UAE, EFTA and the EU, India aims to anchor supply chains in semiconductors and green tech — directly offsetting risks flagged in energy and fertiliser security.

India's fertiliser sector reforms: Time for action

AgriculturePolicyAmitabh Kant

West Asia conflict and Hormuz disruptions have exposed India's fertiliser vulnerability, writes former NITI Aayog CEO Amitabh Kant. The subsidy bill surged to over ₹2.31 trillion in 2023-25 — more than double estimates — rising from ₹75,067 crore in 2014-15 to ₹1.77 trillion in 2024-25, now about 4% of the Union budget.

Urea, sold at a fixed ₹242 per 45kg bag since 2018, accounts for nearly two-thirds of the subsidy and drives overuse. India's NPK use ratio has worsened to 10:9:1 versus the ideal 4:2:1, with urea efficiency only 30-40%. Kant argues for bringing urea under the Nutrient-Based Subsidy regime and expanding Direct Benefit Transfer to farmers via digitised land records and Aadhaar.

He pitches green ammonia — made from renewable hydrogen — as the long-term fix. SECI's recent 724,000-tonne annual tender signals momentum. Despite being the world's third-largest producer, India remains hostage to imported feedstocks.

Why it matters: Fertiliser shocks directly feed food inflation and fiscal strain. Moving to NBS and DBT would cut distortions and leakages, while green ammonia could simultaneously lower subsidy, emissions and import dependence — aligning farm policy with India's energy transition.
