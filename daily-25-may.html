Trump touts imminent Iran agreement to reopen Hormuz

US-IranHormuzGeopolitics

Washington and Tehran have "largely negotiated" a memorandum of understanding to end the three-month war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, President Donald Trump said on Truth Social on 24 May. The Pakistan-brokered draft would address the shipping chokepoint whose closure since February has driven a global energy crisis.

Trump stated the US blockade "will remain in full force and effect until an agreement is reached, certified and signed," adding: "Our relationship with Iran is becoming a much more professional and productive one. They must understand, however, that they cannot develop or procure a Nuclear Weapon or Bomb."

Framework reported by Axios and Reuters:

  • Three-stage process — (1) formally end war, (2) resolve Hormuz crisis, (3) 30-day window (extendable) for broader deal
  • US lifts blockade on Iranian ports and waives some oil sanctions
  • Iran commits never to pursue nuclear weapons; uranium stockpile to be negotiated within 30-60 days
  • Strait to reopen "immediately... without tolls" after US lifts blockade, per Senator Rubio's briefing
  • Phase-one release of some Iranian funds frozen globally

Iran's Tasnim agency said differences remain on "one or two clauses" and warned there would be no final understanding if the US "continued to create obstacles." A senior Iranian source told Reuters the Supreme National Security Council must approve before sending to Ayatollah Khamenei. A Khamenei advisor told Reuters Iranian management of Hormuz "ends 50 years of insecurity in the Gulf."

Israeli politician Benny Gantz called a Lebanon ceasefire a mistake as IDF operations against Hezbollah continue. Pakistani sources say if the memorandum is accepted, detailed talks could resume after Eid ends Friday.

India angle: Hormuz handles ~20% of global oil. Reopening would ease Brent (still up 36% since Feb, near $101), cut insurance premia, and reduce pressure on the rupee — critical after RBI's record $53.1bn net dollar sales in FY26 and $8bn weekly reserve drawdown in mid-May.

Ebola spreads across Congo; flights halted

DRCPublic HealthEbola

The Democratic Republic of Congo suspended all commercial, private and special flights to Bunia in Ituri province on 23 May as the Bundibugyo-strain Ebola outbreak accelerated across three eastern provinces.

Health ministry data as of Friday: 91 confirmed infections, 867 suspected cases, 204 probable deaths. Contact tracing is overwhelmed — health workers have reached only about 350 of 1,745 listed contacts (20%), a gap officials described as "alarming."

The outbreak is now the second-largest on record after the 2014-16 West Africa epidemic. Unlike the Zaire strain, Bundibugyo has no approved vaccine or monoclonal antibody treatment. Officials say hopes for cross-protection from existing vaccines have "faded in recent weeks."

Bunia sits near the Ugandan border, raising cross-border risks that prompted regional health ministers to issue warnings. The US CDC on Saturday expanded enhanced airport screening for travellers arriving from Congo, Uganda and South Sudan.

Local authorities are relying on classic containment — isolation, safe burials, community engagement — but supplies are running low in Ituri and North Kivu, where conflict has already weakened health infrastructure.

Why it matters: For India, beyond humanitarian concern, watch for (1) potential WHO travel advisories affecting Indian pharma and mining personnel in East Africa, (2) supply-chain checks for critical minerals routed via Uganda-Kenya corridors, and (3) renewed focus on pandemic preparedness funding.

AI makes cybercrime threat worse for businesses

CybersecurityAIIndia

Artificial intelligence is compressing the attacker lifecycle. Fortinet's Threat Landscape Report notes "time-to-exploit" has fallen from a week to 24-48 hours. In 2025, there were 640 billion unauthorised access attempts globally — including 67.65 billion brute-force and 121.99 billion exploitation attempts.

India is seeing sharper growth. Kaspersky recorded 369,445 spyware attacks targeting Indian organisations in 2025, up 72% from 214,407 in 2024. Flashpoint tracked 2.32 million internet posts discussing AI for illicit activity in April 2026 alone.

Top 5 attacks worldwide (2025 share): Supply chain (31%), AI-enabled (30%), Phishing (28%), Cyber espionage (26%), Ransomware (25%).

Top vulnerabilities exposed: Software-based entry (44.5%), software vulnerability (30.9%), weak or absent credentials (27.2%), misconfiguration (21.0%), remote code execution (13.6%).

Gartner forecasts Indian enterprise spending on cyber and information security will reach $3.4 billion in 2026, up 11.7% year-on-year, driven by AI-driven SOC automation, identity security, and cloud posture management.

Implication: With West Asia volatility straining energy and logistics firms, AI-augmented phishing and supply-chain attacks pose heightened operational risk. Boards are being advised to prioritize credential hygiene, patch velocity, and third-party risk reviews over the next two quarters.
